Simulation study of low-velocity impact damage in FRPs

Numerical prediction of matrix cracking, delamination and CAI trends

Published: Springer – VETOMAC 2021 Proceedings

What this work is about

  • Built detailed finite-element models of AS4/8552 laminates to simulate low-velocity impact (LVI) and the resulting barely visible impact damage.
  • Captured intralayer damage (matrix cracking, fiber failure) with a user VUMAT and interlayer damage (delamination) using cohesive zone modelling (traction–separation law, QUADS initiation).
  • Post-impact, simulated Compression-After-Impact (CAI) to study the drop in residual compressive strength with increasing impact energy.
  • Compared key response signatures (force–time, damage patterns) against established literature; obtained the classical peanut-shaped delamination and consistent cracking envelopes.

Impact response at 20 J

A typical LVI event shows a rise to a peak load and a noticeable drop once significant damage initiates. The curve below is representative of the simulated 20 J case.

Modeling approach (in brief)

  • Intralayer damage: Hashin and Puck criteria inside a VUMAT to track matrix/fiber failure; damage variables drive stiffness reduction.
  • Interlayer damage: thin cohesive layers between plies, quadratic nominal-stress initiation (QUADS) and energy-based evolution to form delamination.
  • Impact setup: ABAQUS/Explicit with a rigid hemispherical impactor; refined mesh in the impact zone and coarser mesh away from it.
  • CAI simulation: post-impact damage field transferred to a compression model to estimate residual strength trends.

Matrix cracking envelope (20 J)

Envelopes show layer-wise matrix cracking aligned with local fiber direction and propagating through the thickness.

Delamination pattern (20 J)

Cohesive-layer damage reveals the classical peanut-shaped delamination, oriented with the fiber direction of the lower ply.

Key findings

  • The simulation reproduced the expected force–time signature of LVI and the peanut-shaped delamination.
  • Layer-wise matrix cracking patterns and their orientation agreed with reported trends for similar layups.
  • Residual compressive strength from CAI decreased with impact energy; overall trends matched literature for comparable materials.
  • Threshold load for major damage onset was somewhat conservative in the model—typical when contact stiffness, material data, or manufacturing effects differ from tests.
